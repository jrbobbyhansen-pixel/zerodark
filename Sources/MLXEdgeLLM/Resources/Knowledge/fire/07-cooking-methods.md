# Field Cooking Methods

**Boiling:**
Most reliable for food safety and water purification. Requires container. Any metal can improvised into pot. Clay pot (molded, fired slowly) works. Stone boiling: heat rocks in fire (avoid river rocks — can explode from trapped moisture), drop into water-filled container.

**Direct fire:**
Impale on sharpened stick. Rotate constantly. Minimum: meat until no pink visible internally. All wild game to 165°F equivalent (juices run clear, no pink).

**Ember cooking:**
Wrap food in leaves (large, non-toxic), bury in hot coals. 30–60 minutes depending on size. Root vegetables: excellent. Fish: wrap in clay then bake in coals, clay cracks when done.

**Rock baking:**
Flat rocks heated in fire then used as cooking surface. Fry eggs, flatbread, thin meat. Must preheat rocks slowly to prevent cracking.

**Coal and tin:**
Any metal container with lid placed directly on coals. Add hot coal on top for all-around heat.

**Food safety minimums:**
- All wild game/poultry: cook until juices run clear, no pink (165°F equivalent)
- Fish: flakes easily with pressure, opaque throughout
- Eggs: no running yolk in survival situation
- Plants: boiling destroys most toxins, improves digestibility
- Never eat: road-killed animals (toxin accumulation), animals found dead (botulism risk), raw pork

**Water requirement for cooking:**
Cooking requires more water than eating raw. In water-scarce conditions, eat foods that require no preparation over cooked foods.
