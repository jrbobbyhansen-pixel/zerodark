# Terrain Association and Map Reading

**Contour lines:** Connect points of equal elevation. Close together = steep slope. Far apart = gentle slope. V-shape pointing uphill = valley or drainage. V-shape pointing downhill = ridge or spur. Closed circles = hill or depression.

**Prominent features:**
- Ridge: high ground running between two hills, contour lines form elongated Vs pointing downhill on both sides
- Valley: low ground between ridges, contour lines form Vs pointing uphill
- Saddle: low point between two hills, two sets of concentric circles connected at their narrow ends
- Draw: smaller valley, less prominent than main valley
- Spur: shorter ridge projecting from main ridge

**Staying found:**
Check map against terrain every 500m. Identify what you should see ahead before walking there. If terrain doesn't match expectation – stop and reassess before continuing.

**Handrails:** Linear terrain features parallel to your direction of travel – rivers, roads, ridge lines, power lines. Handrails keep you on track without constant compass use.

**Catching features:** Features perpendicular to travel that signal you've gone too far – road, river, cliff, power line. If you hit your catching feature without hitting your destination, you've overshot.

**Attack points:** Obvious, unambiguous terrain features near your objective. Navigate to attack point first, then final leg to objective.

**Urban terrain association:** Use building patterns, street grid, and landmarks. Count blocks. Note distinctive architecture. Church steeples and elevated structures visible at distance.
