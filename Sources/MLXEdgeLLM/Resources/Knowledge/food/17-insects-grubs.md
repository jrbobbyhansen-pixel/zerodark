# Insects and Grubs as Field Food

Insects are the most calorie-dense, accessible, and sustainable protein source in most environments.

**Nutritional value:**
- Grasshoppers: 20g protein per 100g, 6g fat
- Mealworms: 20g protein, 13g fat per 100g (rivals beef)
- Termites: 14g protein, 3g fat per 100g
- Beetle larvae (grubs): 10–17g protein, 6–21g fat depending on species

**Always avoid:**
- Brightly colored insects (warning coloration = toxic)
- Hairy caterpillars (hairs cause internal hemorrhage)
- Insects with strong unpleasant odor
- Stinging insects (ants, wasps, bees – can eat after removing stinger/acid sac)

**Safe targets:**
- Crickets: lift rocks and logs at night. Remove legs and wings before eating.
- Grasshoppers: catch at dawn when cold and slow. Remove legs, wings, head.
- Termites: break open mounds. Eat raw or roast. Mild flavor, high protein.
- White grubs: in rotting logs, under bark, in soil. C-shaped white larvae. Must cook.
- Earthworms: in moist soil under rocks. Purge by placing on damp surface 1 hour, then cook.
- Ants: collect from mound. Formic acid cooks off during preparation.

**Preparation:**
Roasting: dry heat over coals on flat rock or impaled on twig. Removes most parasites. Improves flavor significantly.
Boiling: effective but requires container. Removes parasites, concentrates protein in broth.
Drying: sun-dry insects for long-term storage. Crush into powder and add to other foods.

**Psychological barrier:**
Mix with plant foods or soup initially. Ground insects unidentifiable in food. Caloric density makes psychological cost worth overcoming.
