# Snake, Spider, and Insect Envenomation

**Snakebite:**
Immobilize limb at or below heart level. Remove rings, watches, constrictive items before swelling begins. Mark leading edge of swelling with pen every 15 minutes (tracks venom spread). Keep patient calm – movement and elevated heart rate spread venom faster.
Do NOT: Suck venom, cut and suck, tourniquet, ice, alcohol, apply electrical shock.
Antivenom is the only effective treatment – trigger evacuation.
Identify snake if safely possible (photo, description) – determines antivenom type.
Pit viper (rattlesnake, copperhead, cottonmouth): tissue destruction, swelling, blood clotting abnormalities.
Coral snake: neurotoxic, minimal local signs, progressive paralysis hours later.

**Spider:**
Black widow: severe muscle cramps 1–3 hours after bite, especially abdomen and back. Chest tightness. Pain at bite minimal. Treatment: rest, pain management, cold pack, medical care for antivenom.
Brown recluse: local tissue necrosis developing 24–72 hours. Progresses to deep ulcer. No antivenom – wound care, prevent secondary infection, evacuate.

**Scorpion:**
Arizona bark scorpion: neurotoxic, severe pain, uncontrolled muscle movement, drooling, difficulty swallowing. Antivenom exists. Other species: painful but not life-threatening. Ice, pain management, evacuate if respiratory distress.

**Anaphylaxis (any sting):**
Signs within 15–30 minutes: hives + wheezing + throat swelling + low blood pressure = anaphylaxis.
Epinephrine 0.3mg IM (EpiPen) immediately. Repeat in 5–15 min if no improvement.
Lay flat, elevate legs. Antihistamine secondary.
