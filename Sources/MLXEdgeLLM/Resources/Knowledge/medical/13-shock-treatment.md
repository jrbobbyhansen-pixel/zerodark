# Shock Treatment

## Shock Physiology

**Shock** = inadequate tissue perfusion, body tissues not receiving sufficient oxygen

### Types of Shock

**Hypovolemic** (blood/fluid loss):
- Most common in field scenarios
- Caused by hemorrhage, burns, dehydration
- Treatment: control bleeding, IV fluids

**Cardiogenic** (heart failure):
- Heart unable to pump effectively
- Caused by heart attack, severe injury
- Treatment: medical facility only

**Septic** (infection):
- Bacterial toxins damage vessels
- Caused by infected wound, untreated illness
- Treatment: antibiotics (field expedient: clean wound)

**Distributive** (vessel dilation):
- Vessels lose tone, unable to maintain pressure
- Treatment: positioning, fluids, medical care

## Shock Progression

### Compensatory Stage
- Body maintains blood pressure
- Heart rate increases (100-120 bpm)
- Skin pale, cool, clammy
- Anxiety, restlessness
- Still reversible with treatment

### Decompensatory Stage
- Blood pressure begins to fall
- Altered mental status, confusion
- Rapid weak pulse (120+ bpm)
- Rapid breathing (20+ breaths/min)
- Organ damage beginning

### Irreversible Stage
- Profound hypotension
- Loss of consciousness
- Multi-organ failure
- Death likely even with intervention

## Field Assessment

**Signs of Early Shock**:
- Heart rate > 100 bpm
- Pale, cool, clammy skin
- Anxiety or agitation
- Rapid breathing
- Thirst

**Signs of Advanced Shock**:
- Altered mental status
- Weak or absent pulse
- Severe hypotension
- Peripheral cyanosis (blue lips/fingers)

## Treatment (Pneumonic: STOP the BLEED + Position)

1. **Stop Hemorrhage** — apply direct pressure, tourniquet
2. **Elevate Legs** — 12 inches (unless spinal/chest injury)
3. **Positioning** — supine on flat surface
4. **Oxygen** — if available
5. **Reassurance** — calm, confident demeanor
6. **Fluids** — oral fluids if conscious, able to swallow

## Positioning for Shock

**Standard Shock Position**:
- Supine (lying flat)
- Legs elevated 12 inches
- Do NOT use pillow under head (reduces venous return)

**Exceptions**:
- Spinal injury suspected: keep flat
- Chest/abdominal injury: semi-recumbent (45°)
- Head injury: elevate 30°
- Pregnant patient: left lateral position

## Fluid Resuscitation (Field Expedient)

**Indications**:
- Conscious, no spinal injury
- Able to swallow
- Mild-moderate shock signs

**Technique**:
1. Small frequent sips (avoid large amounts)
2. Water or sports drinks (electrolytes helpful)
3. Watch for vomiting (stop fluids if present)

**Amount**: titrate to patient response (thirst, mental clarity)

## Preventing Shock Development

1. **Control hemorrhage** — direct pressure, dressing
2. **Maintain temperature** — prevent hypothermia (worsens shock)
3. **Minimize movement** — reduce oxygen demand
4. **Reassurance** — anxiety worsens tachycardia
5. **Elevate legs** — promote central circulation

## Monitoring Shock Patients

**Vital Signs Trending**:
- Check pulse every 5-10 minutes
- Note changes in: heart rate, respiratory rate, mental status, skin color
- Deterioration indicates shock progression
- Improvement indicates response to treatment

**Mental Status**:
- Alert and oriented (A&O x 4): normal
- Confused (A&O x 3): early shock
- Lethargic (A&O x 2): moderate shock
- Unresponsive (A&O x 1 or 0): severe shock

## Special Situations

### Anaphylactic Shock
- Severe allergic reaction
- Symptoms: difficulty breathing, wheezing, angioedema
- Treatment: epinephrine IM (if available), oxygen, IV fluids, positioning

### Traumatic Shock
- Multiple injuries, massive bleeding
- Control all hemorrhage aggressively
- Rapid transport critical
- Reassurance essential

### Dehydration-Related Shock
- Inadequate fluid intake in hot environment
- Prevention: early hydration, shade
- Treatment: slow oral rehydration if conscious

## Evacuation Considerations

**Shock patients require rapid medical evaluation**:
- Early evacuation reduces mortality
- Continued deterioration despite treatment = move to help
- Delay acceptable only if immediate severe hemorrhage present

**Transport Position**:
- Continue shock position during evacuation
- Monitor vital signs
- Prevent further injuries during movement
