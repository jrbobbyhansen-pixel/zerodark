# Airway Management

## Airway Anatomy

- **Nasopharynx** — posterior to nasal cavity
- **Oropharynx** — back of mouth
- **Larynx** — voice box, contains vocal cords
- **Trachea** — windpipe, leads to lungs
- **Epiglottis** — flap covering larynx during swallowing

## Basic Airway Positioning

### Recovery Position
1. Turn patient on side
2. Extend lower arm underneath head
3. Bend upper leg for stability
4. Monitor breathing continuously

**Benefits**: keeps airway open, allows fluids to drain from mouth

### Head Tilt-Chin Lift
1. Place hand on forehead
2. Tilt head back gently
3. Lift chin with fingers under lower jaw
4. Opens airway for unconscious patients

**Caution**: avoid excessive head extension (may close airway)

## Nasopharyngeal Airway (NPA)

**Indication**: patient with altered consciousness, gag reflex present

**Sizing**:
- Measure from nostril to earlobe
- Usual size 7-8 mm for adults

**Insertion Technique**:
1. Lubricate with water-based lubricant if available
2. Insert bevel first into nostril
3. Advance along floor of nose
4. Stop when flange reaches nostril
5. Tape in place

**Contraindications**:
- Basilar skull fracture (trauma to face/ears)
- Severe epistaxis
- Nasal obstruction

## Oral Airway (Oropharyngeal Airway)

**Indication**: unconscious patient, no gag reflex

**Sizing**: measure from corner of mouth to earlobe

**Insertion Technique**:
1. Place on side of mouth to confirm size
2. Insert upside down to roof of mouth
3. Rotate 180° as it advances
4. Final position: behind tongue

**Contraindications**: patent gag reflex (causes vomiting/aspiration)

## Choking Management (Heimlich Maneuver)

1. Stand behind patient
2. Wrap arms around waist
3. Make fist above navel, below ribcage
4. Clasp other hand over fist
5. Perform quick upward thrusts
6. Repeat until object dislodges or patient unconscious

**For Unconscious Patient**:
1. Begin CPR cycles (30 compressions)
2. Before rescue breaths, check mouth
3. Remove visible object if found
4. Resume CPR

## Tension Pneumothorax Recognition

**Signs**:
- Severe respiratory distress
- Tracheal deviation (toward opposite side)
- Jugular vein distension
- Hypotension

**Field Treatment** (if trained):
- Needle decompression: 14-16 gauge needle at 2nd intercostal space, midclavicular line
- Mark needle position
- Definitive treatment in medical facility required

## Aspiration Prevention

- Keep patient on side (if safe)
- Monitor airway for blood, vomit
- Clear visible obstruction with finger sweep
- Avoid aggressive chin lift if suspected epiglottitis

## Breathing Assessment

**Look, Listen, Feel**:
1. **Look** — chest rise and fall
2. **Listen** — breath sounds at mouth/nose
3. **Feel** — air movement on cheek
4. Duration: 10 seconds maximum

**Normal Rate**:
- Adult: 12-20 breaths per minute
- Child: 20-30 breaths per minute
- Infant: 40-60 breaths per minute

## Rescue Breathing Technique

1. Open airway (head tilt-chin lift)
2. Pinch nose closed
3. Seal lips around mouth
4. Give 1-second breath
5. Watch for chest rise
6. Allow passive exhalation

**Breaths per minute**: 10-12 for adults

## Breathing Difficulties (Dyspnea)

**Common Causes**:
- Airway obstruction
- Chest trauma
- Pneumonia/infection
- Anxiety/panic
- Altitude

**Field Management**:
- Position upright if conscious
- Calm reassurance
- Oxygen if available
- Seek medical help

## Unconscious Breathing Support

- Position supine or recovery position
- Maintain open airway
- Monitor breathing
- Be ready for rescue breathing
- Do NOT delay other treatment (hemorrhage control)
