# Heat Injury and Treatment

## Heat Injury Progression

### Heat Cramps
- Painful muscle spasms
- Caused by electrolyte depletion, dehydration
- Core temperature normal
- Most benign form

### Heat Exhaustion
- Heavy sweating, weakness, dizziness
- Nausea, possible vomiting
- Core temperature elevated (39-40°C / 102-104°F)
- Still sweating (body trying to cool)

### Heat Stroke
- Altered mental status, confusion
- No sweating (dangerous sign)
- Core temperature very high (>40°C / >104°F)
- Rapid heart rate, rapid breathing
- Medical emergency

## Heat Cramps Treatment

1. Stop activity immediately
2. Move to shade
3. Stretch affected muscle gently
4. Drink electrolyte solution (sports drink, salt water)
5. Apply mild pressure
6. Usually resolves in minutes with rest/hydration

## Heat Exhaustion Treatment

### Cooling Methods
1. Move to cool environment (shade, water)
2. Remove excess clothing
3. Apply cold water (mist, sponge)
4. Drink cool fluids if conscious (sports drinks best)
5. Lie down, elevate legs slightly

### Monitoring
- Core temperature should decrease
- Sweating should continue
- Monitor for progression to heat stroke

### Duration
- Usually improves within 30 minutes
- Continue cooling if symptoms persist
- Seek medical care if not improving

## Heat Stroke Emergency Treatment

**Heat stroke is medical emergency — aggressive cooling critical**

### Immediate Cooling
1. **Cold water immersion** (fastest method)
   - Immerse in cold water bath if available
   - Reduces core temperature rapidly
   - Most effective field treatment

2. **Ice application**
   - Pack ice around groin, armpits, neck (major vessel areas)
   - Rapid cooling without full immersion

3. **Evaporative cooling**
   - Wet body with water
   - Air flow increases evaporation
   - Good field expedient method

### Airway Management
- Position supine
- Head tilt-chin lift (maintain airway)
- Monitor breathing
- Prepare for rescue breathing

### Fluid Management
- If conscious: cold fluids by mouth
- If unconscious: do NOT give fluids
- Once cooled and conscious: rehydrate with electrolytes

### Duration of Cooling
- Continue until core temperature decreases
- Check mental status improvement
- Usually 30 minutes of aggressive cooling

## Preventing Heat Injury

### Acclimatization
- Gradual heat exposure (1-2 weeks)
- Improves heat tolerance
- Critical before intense activity

### Hydration Strategy
- Drink early, drink often
- Don't wait until thirsty
- Electrolyte replacement (sports drinks)
- Avoid alcohol, caffeine

### Activity Modification
- Reduce intensity in heat
- Take frequent breaks in shade
- Avoid midday heat exposure
- Wear light-colored, loose clothing

### Monitoring Self and Others
- Watch for confusion, poor coordination
- Check on companions regularly
- Report symptoms early

## Heat Index Effects

At 95°F ambient:
- 40% humidity: feels like 100°F
- 60% humidity: feels like 110°F
- 80% humidity: feels like 123°F

Higher humidity reduces evaporative cooling (body's main cooling method).

## Exertion in Heat

**Modify activity based on temperature**:
- <75°F: minimal risk
- 75-82°F: moderate risk (increase fluids, rest breaks)
- 82-89°F: high risk (reduce intensity, increase rest)
- >89°F: very high risk (limit activity to essential)

## Medications Increasing Heat Risk

- Antihistamines (reduce sweating)
- Anticholinergics (reduce sweating)
- Diuretics (increase fluid loss)
- Stimulants (increase metabolism)

## Special Populations

### Elderly
- Diminished thermoregulation
- May not recognize heat illness
- Higher mortality from heat stroke

### Children
- Less efficient sweating
- Higher metabolic rate
- Lose heat/gain heat more quickly

### Overweight Individuals
- Reduced heat dissipation
- Higher risk of heat illness

## Recovery After Heat Illness

- Rest in cool environment 24 hours
- Continued hydration
- Avoid intense activity for 24-48 hours
- Heat sensitivity increases (higher risk of recurrence)

## Field vs. Medical Treatment

**Field Treatment Appropriate**:
- Heat cramps (self-limited)
- Heat exhaustion (responds to cooling/hydration)

**Medical Care Needed**:
- Heat stroke (always)
- Heat exhaustion not improving in 30 minutes
- Any altered mental status

Heat stroke carries risk of permanent organ damage — aggressive cooling and medical evaluation critical.
