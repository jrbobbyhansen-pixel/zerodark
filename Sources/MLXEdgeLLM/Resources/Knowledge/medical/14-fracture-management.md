# Fracture Management

## Fracture Types

**Closed Fracture**: skin intact, bone broken internally
**Open (Compound) Fracture**: broken bone pierces skin, high infection risk

**Stable Fracture**: bone fragments held in place
**Unstable Fracture**: bone fragments move easily, high complication risk

## Fracture Assessment

**Signs and Symptoms**:
- Pain, especially with movement
- Swelling, bruising
- Deformity or angulation
- Loss of function
- Crepitus (grinding sensation)

**Neurovascular Check**:
- Distal pulse present?
- Distal capillary refill (< 2 seconds)?
- Sensation present distally?
- Color normal distally (not pale/purple)?

## Splinting Principles

### Goal of Splinting
1. Immobilize fracture site
2. Prevent movement that increases injury
3. Reduce pain
4. Maintain circulation distally
5. Prevent closed fracture from becoming open

### Splinting Technique
1. Immobilize joint above and below fracture
2. Pad splint to prevent pressure sores
3. Check distal pulse before and after
4. Secure with multiple support points
5. Allow some flexibility (not concrete-like)

## Types of Field Splints

### SAM Splint (Structural Aluminum Malleable Splint)
- Lightweight, reusable
- Molds to anatomy
- Excellent field standard
- Carry in pack

### Improvised Splints
- Rolled newspaper, cardboard
- Cloth wrapped around limb
- Traction splint using rope/belt
- Anatomic splint (arm against body, leg against other leg)

### Traction Splint
- Used for femur (thighbone) fractures
- Reduces pain, improves circulation
- Requires two people to apply
- Field expedient: axillary pull

## Specific Fractures

### Arm Fractures
- Sling for immobilization (arm against body)
- SAM splint for additional support
- Assess hand circulation (color, temperature)

### Leg Fractures
- Tie affected leg to unaffected leg
- Elevate if possible (reduce swelling)
- Consider traction for femur fractures

### Rib Fractures
- Tape or wrap chest (prevents splinting)
- Encourage deep breathing (prevent pneumonia)
- Pain management critical
- Monitor for pneumothorax

### Spinal Fractures
- Immobilize entire spine
- Log roll (keep spine straight)
- Backboard or board improvised
- Minimize movement completely

## Pain Management

**Field Expedient Options**:
- Rest, elevation
- Cold application (if available)
- Distraction techniques
- Breathing exercises
- Ibuprofen if safe (check for contraindications)

**Do NOT**:
- Give analgesics that alter consciousness (mask shock/complications)
- Apply heat initially (increases swelling)

## Swelling Management

**RICE Protocol**:
- **Rest** — minimize movement
- **Ice** — cool water if available (20 minutes on/off)
- **Compression** — wrap fracture firmly
- **Elevation** — raise above heart level

## Neurovascular Complications

### Compartment Syndrome
- Swelling in fascial compartments cuts off circulation
- Pain out of proportion to injury
- Limb cyanosis, numbness
- Field treatment: loosen splint, elevate, cool

### Vascular Injury
- Pale, cold distal limb
- Weak or absent distal pulse
- Tingle/numbness progressing
- Careful limb positioning, monitor circulation

## Open Fractures (Special Considerations)

1. Control hemorrhage first
2. Note contamination (for medical providers)
3. Don't replace bone (might introduce contamination)
4. Cover wound with clean dressing
5. Splint fracture
6. Prevent infection (clean wound if water available)

## Fracture Movement During Evacuation

- Splint must be secure enough for transport
- Support limb during movement
- Use sling or carry system for upper extremity
- Consider litter/stretcher for lower extremity
- Pain control priority

## Recovery Timeline

- Typical fracture healing: 6-8 weeks
- Immobilization duration: similar to healing time
- Rehabilitation begins after immobilization
- Full functional recovery: varies by location/severity

## Prevention

- Proper footwear for terrain
- Avoid overloaded pack (affects balance)
- Know limits (avoid dangerous terrain)
- Maintain fitness (strength, flexibility, balance)
- Use trekking poles (reduce leg strain)
