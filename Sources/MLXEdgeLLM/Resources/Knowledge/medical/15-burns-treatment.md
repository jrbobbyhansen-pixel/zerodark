# Burns Treatment

## Burn Severity Classification

### Superficial (1st Degree)
- Red, painful, no blistering
- Sunburn example
- Heal in 3-5 days without scarring

### Partial-Thickness (2nd Degree)
- Red, painful, blisters present
- Most severe burns are partial-thickness
- Heal in 2-3 weeks, may scar
- Highest risk for infection

### Full-Thickness (3rd Degree)
- White, charred, painless (nerve destruction)
- Require surgical intervention
- Severe scarring/contracture

## Rule of Nines (Estimating Body Surface Area Burned)

**Rough Guide** (for adults):
- Head: 9%
- Each arm: 9%
- Chest: 18%
- Abdomen: 18%
- Each leg: 18%
- Genitals: 1%

**Significant Burn**: > 15-20% body surface area requires medical evacuation

## Immediate Burn Treatment

### Stop Burning Process
1. Remove from heat source
2. Remove burning clothing
3. Cool with water if available
4. Do NOT submerge (risk of hypothermia, infection)

### Cooling Technique
- Cool water 10-20 minutes
- Avoid ice directly on skin
- Continue cooling if pain relief needed
- Stop if patient becomes cold

## Wound Care

### Cleaning Burned Area
1. Wash with clean water
2. Remove loose skin/debris gently
3. Leave intact blisters (protective barrier)
4. Pat dry with clean cloth

### Dressing
- Antibiotic ointment if available
- Non-stick sterile dressing
- Wrap with clean cloth
- Change dressing every 24 hours

### Infection Prevention
- Clean wound daily
- Watch for increasing redness, warmth, pus
- May need antibiotics in field (if available)

## Special Burn Types

### Chemical Burns
1. Remove contaminated clothing
2. Flush with copious water (flush away chemical, not into other areas)
3. For lime: brush off before water (heat reaction)
4. Continue flushing 10+ minutes
5. Dress as thermal burns

### Inhalation Injury
- Coughing, wheezing, difficulty breathing
- Singed nasal hair, soot in mouth
- Respiratory distress developing
- Monitor airway, be prepared for rescue breathing
- Evacuation critical

### Electrical Burns
- Entry and exit wounds
- Deep tissue damage likely
- Cardiac arrhythmias possible
- Check pulse, monitor for shock

## Pain Management

**Options**:
- Cool water application (analgesia)
- Elevation (reduces swelling, pain)
- Ibuprofen (if conscious, no contraindications)
- Distraction, breathing exercises

## Fluid Management

### Hypovolemia (Dehydration) Risk
- Large burns cause massive fluid loss
- Field: increase oral fluid intake if conscious
- Monitor for shock (rapid heart rate, pale, cool)
- Elevate legs if signs of shock

### Calculation (for medical reference)
- Parkland Formula: 4 mL × %TBSA × weight (kg) over 24 hours
- First half over 8 hours, second half over 16 hours
- Field: approximate with available fluids

## Burn-Specific Complications

### Infection
- Burned tissue is dead, high infection risk
- Keep dressing clean, change daily
- Watch for fever, increasing redness
- Prophylactic antibiotics if available

### Contracture
- Scar tissue shortens, reduces movement
- Prevent with range-of-motion exercises
- Splint to prevent deformity
- Long-term rehabilitation needed

### Shock
- Large burns cause massive internal bleeding
- Fluid shifts into burned areas
- Aggressive fluid resuscitation critical
- Elevate legs, monitor vital signs

## Special Considerations

### Circumferential Burns
- Burns wrapping entire limb/chest
- Risk of compartment syndrome
- May need escharotomy (surgical division)
- Monitor distal circulation closely

### Face Burns
- Airway threat (swelling narrows airway)
- Monitor breathing closely
- Prepare for airway intervention
- Facial scarring likely

### Radiation Exposure
- Sun exposure burns: treat as thermal
- Ionizing radiation: seek medical care

## Long-Term Care

- Scar management (massage, silicone)
- Range-of-motion exercises
- Pressure garments for scarring
- Psychological support (significant disfigurement)

## Evacuation Criteria

- Any full-thickness burns
- Partial-thickness > 15% TBSA
- Electrical burns (deep tissue damage)
- Chemical burns (ongoing damage risk)
- Burns to face, hands, genitals
- Inhalation injury
