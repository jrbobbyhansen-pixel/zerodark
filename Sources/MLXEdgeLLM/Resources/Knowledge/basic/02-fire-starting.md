# Fire Starting

## Fire Materials (Tinder, Kindling, Fuel)

### Tinder (catches spark/flame easily)
- Dry grass, leaves, bark shreds
- Cattail down, milkweed fluff
- Char cloth (pre-made for reliability)
- Birch bark, cedar bark
- Lint from clothing
- Dead pine needles

### Kindling (pencil-thin to finger-thick)
- Small dry branches
- Split wood (inner wood drier than outside)
- Dry grass bundles
- Pine cones, twigs

### Fuel (sustains fire)
- Split logs (easier to light than rounds)
- Dead branches (prefer standing dead over ground wood)
- Avoid green wood (high moisture)
- Hardwoods burn hotter/longer than softwoods

## Friction Fire

### Bow Drill
- Bow: curved branch with paracord/inner bark
- Spindle: straight, pencil-thick, friction-fit vertical
- Board: flat, softwood, with depression for spindle
- Hearth: notch at edge where ember drops
- Rapid horizontal motion builds heat; correct pressure critical

### Hand Drill
- Spindle and board same as bow drill
- Vertical pressure applied by hands, rolling motion
- More difficult; requires strength and endurance
- Useful for practice

## Flint and Steel

- Strike steel (or metal edge) against flint at 30° angle
- Sparks directed onto char cloth (tinder)
- Char cloth catches sparks easily; transfer to tinder bundle
- Most reliable non-chemical method in field

## Fire Lays (Structure)

### Teepee
- Kindling and fuel arranged cone-shape around tinder
- Good airflow; easy to add fuel
- Collapses easily if tinder not hot enough

### Log Cabin
- Kindling stacked square/rectangular around tinder
- More stable; good for long-duration fires
- Requires more initial material

### Lean-to
- Large fuel log with kindling/tinder below
- Good for windy conditions
- Reflects heat forward

## Fire Management

- Keep tinder dry (plastic bag in pack)
- Build fire in wind-sheltered location
- Never leave fire unattended
- Fully extinguish before leaving (water, stir, check temperature)
