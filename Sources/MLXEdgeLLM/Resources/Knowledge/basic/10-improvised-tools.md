# Improvised Tools

## Stone Knapping (Flint Knapping)

### Basic Flaking Technique
1. Select flint/obsidian (sharp when fractured)
2. Strike along edge with harder stone at 30-45° angle
3. Flakes detach creating sharp blade edges
4. Shape into tool through repeated controlled strikes

### Tools Created
- **Sharp flakes**: cutting edges for hide, plant material
- **Hand axe**: two-sided cutting tool
- **Scrapers**: handle with thumb, edge outward
- **Points**: spear/arrow tips with sharp edges

### Material Selection
- Flint, obsidian, chert: best fracture predictably
- Quartz: works but shatters unpredictably
- Avoid wet stone (moisture reduces control)

## Bone Tool Production

### Materials Available
- Antler (hard, strong, carves cleanly)
- Large bone (ribs, leg bones split easily)
- Fish bone (small, strong for barbs)

### Shaping Techniques
1. Soak in water to soften slightly
2. Carve with sharp stone
3. Snap over edge of rock for angle control
4. Smooth with sand/fine stone

### Tools Produced
- **Fish hooks**: carved curve with barb
- **Spear points**: tapered point with barb
- **Needles**: thin bone sharpened to point
- **Harpoon heads**: barbed multi-point heads

## Woodworking and Shaft Making

### Straight Shafts
1. Select young flexible wood (willow, ash)
2. Heat over fire to straighten
3. Scrape with sharp stone to smooth
4. Dry completely before use

### Hafting (Attaching Point to Shaft)
1. Split end of wooden shaft
2. Insert stone/bone point into split
3. Wrap tightly with cordage/sinew
4. Use tree sap or pitch as adhesive

### Tool Types
- **Digging stick**: hardened point, full-length tool
- **Spear**: attached point with barbs
- **Bow staff**: curved wood with notches for string
- **Club**: weighted end, handle for striking

## Cordage Twisting

### Plant-Based Materials
1. Collect inner bark (oak, elm, willow)
2. Soak 24 hours to soften fibers
3. Pound to break down material
4. Separate into strands
5. Twist 2-3 strands together in Z-pattern
6. Ply multiple twisted strands together

### Sinew Processing (from animals)
1. Remove tendons from meat
2. Dry completely (3-7 days)
3. Pound to separate fibers
4. Twist together like plant cordage
5. Much stronger than plant cordage

### Strength Testing
- Test knot with small load first
- Look for fraying or slipping
- Replace if damage evident

## Primitive Knife/Cutting Tool

### Simple Flake Knife
1. Strike flint to produce sharp flake
2. Wrap base with cloth/leather to protect hand
3. Use edge for cutting meat, plants
4. Replace when dulled (easy to produce new flakes)

### Bone Cutting Tool
1. Select rib bone, split lengthwise
2. Sharpen edge on stone
3. Maintain sharpness by rubbing on stone
4. Lasts longer than flint flakes

## Container Making

### Bark Baskets
1. Strip bark from birch/willow
2. Fold into container shape
3. Weave edges to secure
4. Use for storing food, water collection

### Wooden Bowls
1. Find suitable log
2. Hollow out with fire and scraping
3. Smooth interior with stone
4. Waterproof with hot pitch/sap

### Water Tight Containers
- Seal bark baskets with pitch
- Use clay if available
- Weave tightly to minimize seepage

## Tool Maintenance

- Keep sharp stones/tools dry
- Wrap bone/antler tools in cloth (prevent drying/cracking)
- Store cordage in cool, dry location
- Replace damaged tools promptly

## Typical Survival Tool Kit

- **Flint/obsidian**: 2-3 pieces for knapping
- **Bone fragments**: for carving hooks/points
- **Cordage**: minimum 50 feet
- **Wooden shaft**: for spear/digging stick
- **Bark container**: for water/food storage
- **Sharp stones**: for general cutting

## Time Investment

Creating basic tools requires:
- **Sharp flake**: 5-10 minutes
- **Bone fish hook**: 30-60 minutes
- **Spear with hafted point**: 1-2 hours
- **Cordage (50 feet)**: 2-4 hours
- **Wooden container**: 2-4 hours

Prioritize tools by immediate need (water collection, fire starting, food procurement).
