# Shelter Construction

## Shelter Priorities

Shelter protects from:
- Wind (increases heat loss)
- Precipitation (hypothermia risk)
- Sun exposure (dehydration)
- Insects (infection risk)
- Predators (psychological safety)

Priority: temperature regulation, then weather protection, then comfort.

## Lean-To

**Time to build**: 30 minutes to 2 hours

**Materials**: 1 large pole, 4-6 supports, branches/logs for roof frame, insulation (leaves, evergreen boughs)

**Construction**:
1. Find two trees 8-10 feet apart, or drive two forked uprights
2. Lay main pole (8-10 feet long) across the forks at 6-8 feet height
3. Lean branches at 45° angle from main pole to ground
4. Layer insulation thickly (18+ inches): leaves, pine boughs, bark
5. Open side faces away from wind

**Advantages**: quick, requires minimal tools, good for group shelter

## Debris Hut

**Time to build**: 1-2 hours

**Materials**: long ridge pole, branches, debris (leaves, branches, pine needles), insulation

**Construction**:
1. Find or build ridgepole (8-12 feet, 4+ inches diameter)
2. Prop on low supports or fallen tree (3-4 feet high)
3. Lean branches on both sides at 45° angle
4. Cover frame with evergreen boughs, bark, branches
5. Fill interior with leaves/debris for insulation (18+ inches thick)
6. Create small entrance to minimize heat loss

**Advantages**: enclosed protection, good insulation, minimal materials needed

## Snow Shelter

**Time to build**: 1-3 hours (varies by snow depth/quality)

**Basic snow cave**:
1. Find compacted snowdrift or slope
2. Dig horizontal entrance tunnel, then chamber above
3. Smooth ceiling to prevent dripping
4. Ventilation hole near top (prevents carbon dioxide buildup)
5. Platform in chamber 12-18 inches above floor

**Quinzee** (snow dome):
1. Pile snow 6-8 feet diameter, 6 feet high
2. Wait 24 hours for consolidation
3. Carve entrance and sleeping platform
4. Ventilation hole at top

**Advantages**: excellent insulation (can maintain 0°C inside from body heat), wind protection

## Site Selection Criteria

**Good**: near water, high ground (drainage), protected windbreak, dry soil, accessible fuel

**Bad**: low spots (cold air settles), dead trees overhead (widow-makers), insect nests, near animal trails

## Insulation

Critical for temperature retention:
- Evergreen boughs (pine, spruce) create air pockets
- Bark strips add insulation layer
- Leaves (18+ inches) provide good insulation
- Moss acts as natural insulation
- Create barrier between body and ground

## Bedding

Elevated bedding (6+ inches) insulates from ground:
- Build frame of branches
- Layer leaves, grass, pine boughs
- Use clothing as additional insulation
- Never sleep directly on ground (conducts heat away)
