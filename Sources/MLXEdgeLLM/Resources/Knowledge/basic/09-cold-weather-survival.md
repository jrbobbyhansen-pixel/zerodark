# Cold Weather Survival

## Hypothermia Recognition and Treatment

### Stages of Hypothermia

**Mild (32-35°C / 90-95°F)**
- Shivering, confusion, poor coordination
- Slurred speech, irrational decisions
- Still capable of performing tasks with effort

**Moderate (28-32°C / 82-90°F)**
- Shivering decreases/stops
- Severe confusion, drowsiness
- Loss of fine motor control
- May refuse help ("paradoxical undressing")

**Severe (Below 28°C / Below 82°F)**
- Unconsciousness likely
- Muscle rigidity
- Barely detectable pulse/breathing
- May appear dead ("no one is dead until warm and dead")

### Treatment

**Field Expedient**:
1. Move to shelter immediately
2. Remove wet clothing
3. Insulate from ground
4. Apply external heat: body-to-body contact, warm drinks (if conscious)
5. Avoid sudden movement (risk of cardiac arrhythmia)

**Do NOT**:
- Massage limbs (causes afterdrop)
- Apply intense direct heat
- Give alcohol or caffeine
- Submerge in hot water (risk of shock)

## Layering System

### Base Layer (Moisture Wicking)
- Merino wool, synthetic (NOT cotton)
- Pulls sweat away from skin
- Maintains insulation when damp

### Insulating Layer
- Down, synthetic insulation, wool
- Traps air, prevents convection heat loss
- Thicker layer for colder temperatures

### Wind/Weather Layer
- Waterproof, windproof shell
- Allows moisture escape (breathable)
- Prevents convection cooling

## Cold-Related Injuries

### Frostbite Prevention
- Maintain dry extremities
- Keep core body temperature high
- Layer adequately, avoid tight clothing (restricts circulation)
- Exercise regularly to maintain circulation

### Frostbite Treatment
1. Move to warm shelter
2. Gradual rewarming (40°C water for 20-40 minutes)
3. Avoid freezing/thawing cycles
4. Elevate affected limbs
5. Seek medical care (blistering, tissue death require professional evaluation)

### Trench Foot (Non-Freezing Cold Injury)
- Caused by prolonged exposure to cold, damp conditions
- Prevention: keep feet dry, change socks frequently
- Treatment: gradual rewarming, avoid direct heat

## Winter Movement Techniques

### Snow Travel
- Snowshoes reduce sinking in deep snow
- Skis provide faster travel on packed snow
- Gaiters keep snow out of boots

### Pace and Rest
- Reduce pace in cold (conserves energy)
- Take frequent short breaks
- Remove excess clothing to prevent sweating

### Avalanche Awareness
- Avoid slopes steeper than 30°
- Watch for signs: recent weather, unstable snow layers
- Travel one at a time in risky terrain
- Carry avalanche beacon, shovel, probe

## Energy Management in Cold

### Caloric Needs
- Cold increases metabolism 20-50%
- Requires more food (especially fats, carbs)
- Avoid starvation in extended cold exposure

### Activity Level
- Moderate activity maintains core heat
- Excessive activity causes sweating (dangerous when wet)
- Balance activity with rest for steady heat production

## Cold Weather Shelter

### Heat Loss Prevention
- Insulate from ground completely (ground absorbs 50%+ heat)
- Minimize shelter volume (easier to heat)
- Reduce air circulation inside shelter
- Block wind completely

### Emergency Shelter in Cold
- Snow cave provides excellent insulation
- Debris hut with thick insulation works
- Back against windbreak (rock wall, dense forest)

## Winter Survival Priorities

1. **Shelter** (prevents heat loss)
2. **Fire** (active warming source)
3. **Insulation** (proper clothing/bedding)
4. **Calories** (fuel for warmth)
5. **Hydration** (dehydration increases hypothermia risk)
