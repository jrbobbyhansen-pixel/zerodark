# Rope and Knots

## Essential Field Knots

### Bowline (Rabbit Comes Out of the Hole)

**Uses**: fixed loop, secure attachment, won't slip under load

**Tying**:
1. Make small loop in rope (rabbit hole)
2. Pass working end up through loop
3. Wrap around standing line (rabbit around tree)
4. Back down through loop (into hole)
5. Tighten under load

**Strength**: retains ~80% rope strength

### Clove Hitch (Two Half-Hitches)

**Uses**: quick temporary attachment, tying to poles/trees

**Tying**:
1. Wrap around pole, cross over itself
2. Wrap again, pull working end through
3. Creates two interlocking half-hitches

**Strength**: reliable for static loads, slips under shock loading

### Prusik Knot (Mechanical Ascender)

**Uses**: ascend rope, rescue haul systems, friction brake

**Tying**:
1. Loop cord around main rope
2. Wrap loop around main rope 3-4 times
3. Pass end back through original loop
4. Under load, loop cinches and holds
5. Under no load, can slide freely

**Note**: requires 3-4 mm paracord around thicker rope (~10 mm)

### Anchor Hitch (Taut-Line Hitch)

**Uses**: adjustable loop that won't slip, hauling/hoisting

**Tying**:
1. Wrap rope around pole twice
2. Make third wrap around standing part only
3. Pull tight; loop slides until load applied, then holds

### Sheet Bend (Cordage Joiner)

**Uses**: joining two ropes of different diameters

**Tying**:
1. Make loop in thicker rope
2. Pass thinner rope through loop
3. Wrap thinner rope around both strands of loop
4. Pull tight

**Strength**: secure even with different rope sizes

## Field Cordage

### Paracord (550 Paracord)
- Tensile strength: 550 lbs
- Inner strands provide additional cordage
- Versatile, reliable
- Carry 50+ feet in pack

### Natural Cordage (Field Expedient)

**Inner bark (oak, elm, willow)**:
1. Soak bark 24 hours to soften
2. Pound to separate fibers
3. Twist fibers together into strands
4. Braid 3-4 strands into rope
5. Rough cordage for light loads (snares, shelter)

**Vines**: grapevine, dogbane, nettle provide strong fiber

**Grass/plant stems**:
- Cattail leaves can be twisted
- Corn husks create weak cordage
- Rush stems suitable for lashing

## Rope Care and Storage

- Keep dry (mold weakens fibers)
- Avoid sharp edges (causes fraying)
- Coil properly to prevent kinks
- Inspect regularly for damage
- Melt nylon rope ends to prevent fraying

## Load-Bearing Assemblies

### Anchor Points
- Must support 5x intended load (safety factor)
- Solid trees, rock anchors, stakes
- Test before using

### Hauling Systems

**Mechanical advantage 2:1**:
- Single pulley redirects load
- No force multiplication
- Good for changing rope angle

**Mechanical advantage 3:1**:
- One moving pulley, one fixed
- Pull half distance for half force
- Useful for heavy loads

## Knot Failure Modes

- Slipping: knot comes untied under load
- Breaking: rope strength exceeded
- Poor grip: inadequate wraps or hitches
- Environmental: UV damage, abrasion

**Prevention**:
- Use appropriate knot for load type
- Inspect knots before applying force
- Maintain proper tension during tying
- Backup critical knots with secondary knot
