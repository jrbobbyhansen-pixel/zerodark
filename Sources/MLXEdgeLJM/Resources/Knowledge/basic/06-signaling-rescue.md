# Signaling and Rescue

## Signal Mirror

**Most effective long-range signal** (visible 50+ miles in clear conditions)

### Construction
- Any reflective surface: broken mirror, polished metal, CD
- Hole in center (optional, aids aiming)

### Use
1. Reflect sunlight toward target/aircraft
2. Aim at observer, sweep back and forth across target
3. Three flashes = distress code (SOS)
4. Continue signaling as long as aircraft visible

### Advantages
- Requires no batteries, fuel, or consumables
- Highly visible in daylight
- Silent (good for covert situations)

## Fire and Smoke Signals

### Smoke Signal
- Dry vegetation creates white smoke in day
- Green branches or damp materials increase smoke
- Arrange in triangles or rows (international distress symbol)
- Visible for miles in clear conditions

### Signal Fire
- Build three fires in triangle formation (distress signal)
- Use dry wood (efficient burning)
- Keep fuel nearby for rapid attention
- At night, bright flames visible far away

## Ground-to-Air Signals

### Signal Markers
Arrange ground signals (3 elements minimum):
- **SOS** in rocks, branches, or trenches
- **X** (need assistance)
- **V** (require assistance)
- **→** (going this direction)
- Arrows point direction of travel

### Visibility Factors
- Contrast: white cloth on dark ground (or reverse)
- Size: minimum 5-6 feet per element for visibility from aircraft
- Reflective materials increase visibility

## International SERE Codes

Downed pilot or survivor can arrange signal codes for aircraft:

- **V** = require medical assistance
- **X** = require mechanical help
- **F** = require food/water
- **↑** = going this direction
- **N** = negative (no)
- **Y** = affirmative (yes)
- **△** = request pickup

## Sound Signals

### Whistle
- High-pitched, travels far
- Three blasts = distress (SOS)
- Carry emergency whistle in pack

### Shouting
- Carry over shorter distance than whistle
- Use in coordination with visual signals

## Survivor Location Equipment

### Personal Locator Beacon (PLB)
- Battery-powered distress transmitter
- Activates Search and Rescue response
- GPS position transmitted to authorities
- Range: satellite-based, global coverage

### Emergency Reflector Blanket
- Bright orange/silver
- Highly visible, maintains body heat
- Unfold and wave for signal

## Mirror Signaling Practice

1. Aim at target (hill, aircraft) by sighting through finger hole
2. Align sunlight reflection with target
3. Sweep steadily; observer sees flash
4. Continue signaling until confident message received
5. Use reflector sparingly to preserve batteries (if electronic)

## Rescue Expectation

**Establish rescue plan before emergency**:
- File travel plans with others
- Activate PLB/EPIRB if available
- Create visible signals
- Remain at crash/incident site (easier to locate)
- Signal continuously during daylight hours
- Conserve energy and supplies for extended signaling
